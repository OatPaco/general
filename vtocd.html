Evidence-Based Guide

Visual Tourettic OCD

A comprehensive, research-backed guide to self-help strategies and medication options for VTOCD — because understanding it is the first step to reclaiming your life.

scroll to explore ↓

The most effective treatment for VTOCD is a combined approach — Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) blended with Habit Reversal Training / CBIT, often augmented by a high-dose SSRI plus either a low-dose dopamine blocker or an alpha-2 agonist.

Standard OCD treatment alone is frequently inadequate — McDougle et al. (1993) found only a 21% SSRI-monotherapy response rate in OCD-with-tics vs. 52% without.

💡 The single most important shift: stop treating the eye urges as a problem to eliminate (which fuels them) and start treating them as a tic to be tolerated — while you systematically extinguish the OCD-driven checking, avoidance, and rumination. The latter causes the suffering, not the gaze itself.
Research

Key Findings

01

VTOCD is a recognized clinical subtype — but not in DSM-5

Visual Tourettic OCD is a variant of Tourettic OCD (TOCD), introduced by Mansueto & Keuler in their 2005 paper. The 2022 review by Katz et al. (Boston Children's Hospital) confirms TOCD is an "intermediate neuropsychiatric disorder distinct from either TS or OCD alone" with overlapping cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) circuit involvement.

02

Standard OCD treatment is often inadequate

McDougle et al. (1993, 1994) found OCD patients with tics responded to SSRI monotherapy at only 21% vs. 52% for OCD without tics. The POTS study replicated this in youth: sertraline monotherapy did no better than placebo for tic-related OCD.

03

Pharmacology must address both components

Per Katz et al. 2022: "TOCD often warrants combined treatment as well as higher dosing regimens due to overlapping symptomatology. Management with antipsychotics is required more often."

04

Behavioral therapy must be modified

Mansueto & Keuler (2005) state TOCD requires longer ERP regimens, more rote practice, deliberate "just wrong" exposure, muscle relaxation, diaphragmatic breathing, and competing-response substitution strategies — none standard in OCD ERP.

05

VTOCD has its own validated self-help techniques

Dr. Jonathan Grayson developed the "sneak-a-peek" and "three-point gaze" techniques specifically for VTOCD, now standard recommendations from IOCDF grassroots advocates.

Understanding

What VTOCD Actually Is

VTOCD presents in four overlapping patterns:

1 Repetitive involuntary glances or stares at objects in the visual periphery
2 Fear of staring inappropriately at private body areas — without actually doing so
3 Believing one is staring when one is not
4 Actually staring or glancing in ways others notice

The tic component is the involuntary, urge-driven eye movement — often preceded by a "not just right" sensation, similar to a Tourette premonitory urge.

The OCD component is the catastrophic interpretation: "What if I'm a pervert? What if I get fired?" — plus compulsions like avoidance, mental checking, reassurance-seeking, blinking hard to "reset," and wearing sunglasses.

Strategies

Self-Treatment & Coping

A

ERP Modified for VTOCD

The gold standard — but the exposure target is NOT the staring itself; it's the feared consequence of being caught or being a "bad person."

  • Build a fear hierarchy around avoided situations: gym, beach, transit, meetings. Rank each 0–10.
  • Start at the bottom and stay until the urge to escape drops by half (typically 20–45 minutes).
  • Drop the rituals: stop defensive sunglasses, reassurance-seeking, mental reviewing, avoidance, and the "blink-hard reset."
  • Imaginal exposure: write "Someone saw me glance and now thinks I'm a creep" and read it daily until it loses charge.
B

Grayson's "Sneak-a-Peek" Technique

Counterintuitively, give yourself permission to look. The harder you fight the urge, the stronger it gets.

  • At social distance (~10 ft): look → acknowledge → move on, rather than fighting the gaze
  • At far distance: give yourself permission to stare openly — it's not a problem at distance
  • Do quick, deliberate "sneak peeks" rather than fighting and producing the prolonged tic-stare
  • Accept in advance: "Sometimes I stare blankly, excuse me" — and accept being caught as tolerable
C

Three-Point Gaze Training

Focus on an imaginary triangle on the other person's face — chin + a point above each eye. Rotate your gaze among these slowly. This produces natural interest-display without locked staring.

D

Habit Reversal Training (HRT) / CBIT

Recommended as first-line for tics by the 2019 AAN Practice Guideline. Self-applied steps:

  • Awareness training: log every visual tic for one week — time, location, what preceded it
  • Competing response: when you feel the urge, do an incompatible behavior for one minute — e.g., deliberate slow blinking, looking at a fixed neutral point
  • Function-based intervention: identify worsening environments and modify (sit facing a wall, take a longer-distance seat, take breaks)
E

ACT Techniques

  • Cognitive defusion: instead of "I'm a creep," say "I'm having the thought that I'm a creep… and that's just OCD doing its thing." Sing the thought to a silly tune.
  • Acceptance: treat the urge as a transient body event, not a moral signal
  • Values-based action: keep going to social settings because they matter to you, not when anxiety has cleared
  • Self-as-context: you are the awareness in which OCD/tics arise — not the thoughts themselves
F

Mindfulness & Self-Compassion

When the tic happens, place a hand on your chest and say: "This is hard. This is OCD attacking what I value most. I'm doing my best."

Recommended resource: Kimberley Quinlan's The Self-Compassion Workbook for OCD (New Harbinger).

G

Mansueto's Specific TOCD Additions

  • Diaphragmatic breathing during exposures: imagine "breathing away" tension
  • Differential muscle relaxation: target the specific muscle group producing the urge (often eyes, brow, jaw)
  • "Just wrong" practice: deliberately perform tics imperfectly — look slightly off-target, end the glance early — to build tolerance
  • Substitution strategies: a competing motor response such as gently squeezing a fist

What NOT to Do

  • Wear sunglasses defensively (compulsion)
  • Avoid social situations (compulsion)
  • Seek reassurance from friends/partners ("Was that weird?")
  • Compulsively google or post about VTOCD seeking certainty
  • Try to suppress every urge — this often makes them stronger (paradoxical rebound)
  • Take cannabis as self-treatment — it can worsen OCD anxiety in many users
Pharmacology

Medications

⚠ All medication decisions must be made with a psychiatrist. Doses below are typical adult ranges.

First-Line: SSRI Monotherapy (OCD-Level Doses)

SSRIs for OCD require higher doses than for depression, and a longer trial (8–12 weeks minimum). Remember: only 21% response rate expected in OCD-with-tics.

Medication Brand OCD Dose Range
Fluoxetine Prozac 40–80 mg/day (up to 120)
Sertraline Zoloft 200–400 mg/day
Fluvoxamine Luvox 200–300 mg/day (up to 450)
Paroxetine Paxil 40–60 mg/day (up to 100)
Escitalopram Lexapro 10–20 mg/day (up to 60)
Clomipramine Anafranil 100–250 mg/day

Second-Line: SSRI + Low-Dose Antipsychotic

Approximately one-third of treatment-refractory OCD patients respond to antipsychotic augmentation (NNT = 4.6). Patients with comorbid tic disorders respond particularly well.

Medication Brand Augmentation Dose Notes
Risperidone Risperdal 0.5–3 mg/day 32% YGTSS reduction in Yale's RCT
Aripiprazole Abilify 5–15 mg/day 5 mg if <50 kg, 10 mg if ≥50 kg
Haloperidol Haldol 1–3 mg/day FDA-approved for tics; EPS risk
Quetiapine Seroquel ~169 mg/day Less evidence than risperidone
Brexpiprazole Rexulti 1–4 mg/day Emerging evidence; not yet guideline-level

Alpha-2 Agonists (First-Line for the Tic Component)

Lower side-effect burden than antipsychotics and particularly well-suited to TOCD/VTOCD.

Medication Brand Dose Notes
Guanfacine Tenex / Intuniv 1.5–4 mg/day Less sedating; start 0.5 mg at bedtime
Clonidine Catapres 0.1–0.4 mg/day Oral or patch; do not stop abruptly

Action Plan

Staged Approach

Stage 1 — Self-Help Baseline (Start Now)

  1. Read Mansueto & Keuler's 2005 paper and the Katz 2022 review (open access at PMC9363583)
  2. Start a daily 10-minute mindfulness or self-compassion practice
  3. Stop the obvious compulsions: sunglasses, avoidance, reassurance-seeking, mental review, blink-resetting
  4. Begin Grayson's three-point face triangle and sneak-a-peek in low-stakes situations
  5. Build a written fear hierarchy and start exposures at level 2–3
  6. Work through Carol Edwards' Address Staring OCD
  7. Join the IOCDF Visual Tourettic OCD Warrior Badass Tribe support group

Stage 2 — Find a Specialist (Within 4–8 Weeks)

  1. Find a clinician trained in both ERP for OCD and CBIT for tics (IOCDF directory or TAA Behavior Therapy Institute)
  2. Ask: "Are you familiar with Tourettic OCD as described by Mansueto?" — if not, find another
  3. Begin combined ERP + HRT with weekly sessions; expect 16–20+ weeks

Stage 3 — Add Medication (If Moderate-Severe After 8–12 Weeks)

  1. Add an SSRI — sertraline or fluoxetine, titrated to OCD doses over 12 weeks
  2. If partial response: add guanfacine, low-dose aripiprazole, or risperidone

Stage 4 — Treatment-Resistant (<25–35% Y-BOCS Reduction After 3 Months)

  1. Switch SSRIs (try at least 2–3); consider clomipramine
  2. Consider supratherapeutic SSRI dosing under close supervision
  3. Consider referral for TMS or — for severe adult cases — DBS evaluation

Benchmarks That Should Change the Plan

12 weeks of well-implemented ERP+CBIT with no improvement → add or change medication
<25% Y-BOCS reduction after 3 months on therapeutic-dose SSRI → add antipsychotic or alpha-2 agonist
6–9 months of SSRI + augmentation + therapy with persistent impairment → refer to tertiary center
Suicidal ideation, complete social withdrawal, or job loss → urgent psychiatric referral; do not stay in Stage 1 self-help
Important Notes

Caveats

Not in the DSM-5. VTOCD remains a clinical label, not a diagnostic one. Using "tic-related OCD" may communicate better with unfamiliar clinicians.
No RCTs specific to VTOCD. All recommendations are extrapolated from tic-related OCD studies and clinical experience.
Pediatric vs. adult treatment differs. Most TOCD evidence is pediatric; adult VTOCD is under-studied.
Antipsychotic side effects are real. Stay at low doses. The UK Advanced Interventions Service warns against high-dose augmentation (risperidone 6–8 mg/day).
SSRIs in young people require monitoring for treatment-emergent suicidality (FDA black box warning under age 25).
Self-help is not a substitute for treatment when impairment is severe. If you are losing your job, isolating, or having suicidal thoughts, seek immediate professional help.
Online sources vary in quality. Rely on IOCDF.org, Tourette.org, AAN guidelines, PubMed/PMC, and named clinicians.
